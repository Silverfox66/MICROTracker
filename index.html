Context

Why Are There Microplastics in the Charles River?

The Charles River flows through one of the most urbanized areas in Massachusetts, making it especially vulnerable to microplastic pollution. Microplastics are plastic particles smaller than 5 millimeters that come from the breakdown of larger plastic items or are released directly from everyday activities.

One of the biggest sources is stormwater runoff. When it rains, water washes tiny plastic particles from roads, parking lots, and sidewalks into storm drains that flow directly into the river. Tire wear from cars is another major contributor because driving constantly releases microscopic rubber and plastic particles onto the road. Synthetic clothing also sheds tiny plastic fibers during washing, and although wastewater treatment plants capture many of these fibers, some still reach the river. In addition, litter, artificial turf, road paint, and construction materials gradually break down into smaller plastic fragments that enter the watershed.

Once in the Charles River, microplastics can be eaten by fish, insects, and other aquatic organisms. Scientists are still studying their long-term effects, but they may harm wildlife and transport other pollutants through the food web. Reducing plastic waste and preventing runoff are important steps toward protecting the Charles River for future generations.
